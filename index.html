Skin Cancer Statistics Worldwide

-~-

Skin cancer statistics worldwide

Around the globe, there are around 2 to 3 million non-melanoma skin cancer cases that occur every year according to the World Health Organization. Australia has the highest incidence of malignant melanoma cases each year, which is nearly 10 to 20 times higher than many European populations. Worldwide, melanoma of the skin is considered the 17th most common cancer. When divided by gender, it becomes the 15th most common cancer in men and the 19th most common cancer in women. According to the World Cancer Research Fund, New Zealand followed by Norway had the highest rate of melanoma cancer-related deaths in 2020. The chart on the right side of the image shows the nationalities most susceptible to skin cancer, including countries such as New Zealand, Australia, Switzerland, Sweden, Norway, Denmark, the USA, and the UK.

Skin Cancer Statistics In The U.S.

-~-

Skin cancer statistics in the U.S.

One in five people from the United States will develop some form of skin cancer by the age of 70, according to data from the Academy of Dermatology (2018). While skin cancer rates are higher in women than men before the age of 50, after age 50, men have higher rates of skin cancer (American Cancer Society, 2021). Additionally, the incidence of skin cancer among non-Hispanic White individuals is almost 30 times higher than the incidence among non-Hispanic Black or Asian/Pacific Islander individuals (American Cancer Society, 2020).

Skin Cancer Statistics By Type Of Cancer

-~-

Skin cancer statistics by type

The incidence of basal cell carcinoma increased by 145% from 1976–1984 to 2000–2010. During that same timeframe, the incidence of squamous cell carcinoma increased by 263%, according to Mayo Clinic Proceedings (2017). For Black people, the most common form of skin cancer is squamous cell carcinoma (Journal of the Dermatology Nurses' Association, 2009). Furthermore, Current Dermatology Reports (2014) notes that the diagnosis and treatment of non-melanoma skin cancer in the United States increased by 77% between 1994 and 2014.

Melanoma Statistics

-~-

Melanoma statistics

As of 2018, more than 1.3 million Americans were living with melanoma (National Cancer Institute, 2018). It is estimated that in 2022, around 100,000 cases of melanoma will be diagnosed in the United States (CA: A Cancer Journal for Clinicians, 2022). In that same year, melanoma is projected to be the 5th most commonly diagnosed cancer in both men and women (CA: A Cancer Journal for Clinicians, 2022). Despite the growing overall rates of melanoma, the incidence of this cancer in children and adults under the age of 30 has decreased. However, rates of melanoma have increased in older populations, especially among those aged 80 years and older (Journal of the National Cancer Institute, 2019).

Skin Cancer Statistics By Cause

-~-

Skin cancer statistics by cause

Excessive exposure to UV radiation from the sun, the use of tanning beds, and a history of the disease all increase the risk of developing skin cancer (American Cancer Society, 2022). Sunburns during childhood and adolescence increase the odds of developing melanoma later in life. Specifically, those who have five or more blistering sunburns between 15 and 20 years of age will increase their risk of melanoma by 80% and non-melanoma skin cancer by 68% (Cancer Epidemiology, Biomarkers & Prevention, 2014). The use of tanning beds increases the risk of melanoma along with the risk of early-onset melanoma (Preventive Medicine Reports, 2016). Furthermore, women younger than 30 years of age that use indoor tanning beds are six times more likely to develop melanoma (JAMA Dermatology, 2016).

Skin Cancer Survival Rate

-~-

Skin cancer survival rate

The survival rate for common forms of non-melanoma skin cancer is higher than 95% when detected and treated early (Moffitt Cancer Center, 2022). If melanoma is detected and treated before it has spread to the lymph nodes, there is a 99% survival rate. However, the five-year survival rate is 68% for individuals whose melanoma spread to nearby lymph nodes, and it drops to 30% once it spreads to distant lymph nodes and other organs (American Academy of Dermatology Association, 2022). For Black patients with melanoma, the five-year survival rate across all stages of melanoma is only 71%, compared to a 93% five-year survival rate for White patients (CA: A Cancer Journal for Clinicians, 2022).

Skin Cancer Death Rate

-~-

Skin cancer death rate

The statistics show that melanoma remains the primary driver of skin cancer fatalities, with nearly 20 Americans dying from the disease every day. Current estimates from the American Cancer Society (2026) project that annual melanoma deaths will rise to approximately 8,510, with 5,500 of these being men and 3,010 being women. This trend reinforces research indicating that men generally experience lower survival rates compared to women. While the total number of deaths is expected to increase alongside a 10.6% rise in new diagnoses for the year, overall death rates have actually decreased over the last decade by about 2% per year in women and 3% per year in men due to advancements in early detection and treatment.

The Cost Of Skin Cancer

-~-

The cost of skin cancer

While data from the 2007–2011 period established a significant baseline, updated figures for 2024–2026 reveal a growing medical and economic burden. Currently, approximately 6.1 million adults are treated for skin cancer annually in the United States, with roughly 9,500 people diagnosed every single day. The total annual treatment cost has risen to an estimated $8.9 billion, driven largely by the high cost of advanced therapies; for instance, the global melanoma treatment market alone is projected to reach $8.78 billion in 2026. Furthermore, the financial impact on individual patients has intensified, with some modern immunotherapy treatments for advanced melanoma exceeding $190,000 per year. Despite these rising costs, the economic strain attributed specifically to indoor tanning remains a significant contributor to the national healthcare expenditure, continuing to exceed the $343 million annual mark previously identified.
